# Why 'Machine Boss'?

A **political machine** is an organization in which a single leader — the **boss** — controls
a disciplined hierarchy of operatives who deliver votes, patronage, and favors in exchange
for power.

The prototype was **Tammany Hall**, New York's Democratic machine, which dominated city politics
from the mid-nineteenth century through the 1960s. Under bosses like William M. Tweed and later
Charles Francis Murphy, Tammany turned ward-level loyalty into a self-perpetuating engine of
municipal government — and spectacular graft.

The model proved portable. In Chicago, **Richard J. Daley** ran the Cook County Democratic
organization for over two decades (1955--1976), combining iron party discipline with genuine
public-works ambition. Kansas City had Tom Pendergast; Jersey City had Frank Hague;
Atlantic City had **Enoch "Nucky" Johnson**.

Johnson is the real figure behind **Nucky Thompson**, the Prohibition-era boss played by Steve Buscemi
in HBO's *Boardwalk Empire*. The historical Johnson controlled the Atlantic City rackets through
the 1920s and 30s, collected a "cut" from every speakeasy and gambling den on the boardwalk,
and in 1929 helped organize the **Atlantic City Conference** — a landmark sit-down of organized-crime
figures convened in the aftermath of the St. Valentine's Day Massacre. Al Capone attended;
so did Lucky Luciano, Meyer Lansky, and other luminaries of the underworld.
Johnson brokered the meeting on his home turf, a reminder that the boss's real product was
not ideology but *infrastructure*: the network of relationships that let the machine run.

The model of a subservient political machine demonstrating absolute fealty to a crime boss
is now, of course, almost completely forgotten.
